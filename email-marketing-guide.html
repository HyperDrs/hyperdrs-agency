Practical Guide

Test, learn, and improve your marketing

Marketing works best when it's tested, not guessed. Whether you're emailing existing customers or reaching out to new prospects, small changes in timing, wording, and structure can make a big difference in results. Here's a practical breakdown of three things every business should understand: A/B testing, email marketing, and cold outreach.

What is A/B testing—and why does it matter?

A/B testing means showing two versions of something—an email subject line, a webpage headline, or an ad—to two similar groups of people, then measuring which version performs better. One group sees version A, the other sees version B, and the data tells you which one wins.

It matters because intuition is often wrong. You might think a punchy subject line will get more opens, but until you test it against a straightforward one, you're guessing. A/B testing replaces opinions with evidence.

Useful elements to test

  • Email subject lines: short and direct versus curiosity-driven
  • Calls to action, such as “Shop Now” versus “See the Collection”
  • Send times and days
  • Email length: a quick note versus a longer, story-driven message
  • Landing-page headlines and images

How to run a simple test

  1. Pick one variable to change at a time. If you change the subject line and send time together, you won't know which one moved the needle.
  2. Split your audience randomly and evenly.
  3. Run the test long enough to get a meaningful sample. A few hundred recipients per version can be a reasonable starting point for small businesses, though the required sample depends on baseline results and the size of the difference you need to detect.
  4. Let the data decide, then apply the winner to your next campaign.

The goal isn't to test forever. It's to build a habit of testing regularly so your marketing keeps improving instead of standing still.

Why and when to use email marketing

Email can be a high-return marketing channel, especially for businesses with loyal or repeat customers. Unlike social media, where algorithms influence who sees your content, email gives a business a direct way to reach people who have chosen to hear from it.

Why it works

  • You own the list. Social platforms can change their algorithms; your permission-based customer list remains an asset you control.
  • It's highly targeted. Segment customers by purchase history, interests, or location and send something relevant instead of a generic blast.
  • It's cost-effective. Compared with many paid channels, email can deliver strong returns with modest sending costs.
  • It builds relationships over time. Regular, useful emails can build trust in a way a one-time ad impression cannot.

When to use it

  • New product launches or restocks: let subscribers know before posting publicly.
  • Events and community activity: promote group runs, in-store fittings, local races, and seasonal sales.
  • Post-purchase follow-ups: send a thank-you, care tip, or useful product guidance to build loyalty.
  • Re-engagement: reconnect thoughtfully with customers who have not purchased recently.

Email marketing isn't about messaging your entire list every day. It's about showing up consistently with something useful or relevant so people are glad to see your name in their inbox.

Tips for good cold outreach

Cold outreach—emailing someone who hasn't interacted with your business before—is different from email marketing to an existing list. The person doesn't know you yet, so the goal is to earn a reply, not make a sale in one message.

1. Keep it to a short sequence

Don't over-email a cold prospect. A concise initial message and one respectful follow-up can account for a missed first email without becoming a nuisance.

  • Email 1: introduce yourself briefly, explain why you're contacting this business specifically, and offer one clear, low-effort next step.
  • Email 2: send a shorter, friendly follow-up a few days later. Reference the first message, add a small piece of new value, and repeat the call to action.

2. Test your timing

Timing can affect results, but no send window is universal. Tuesday or Wednesday mornings around 9 a.m. can be a useful starting hypothesis for a business audience; test it against another time using the recipient's local time zone.

One possible cadence is an initial message on Tuesday morning and a follow-up the following Wednesday morning. The gap should feel like a natural check-in rather than a repeat.

3. Keep it short and specific

Reference something genuine about the recipient's business, ask one clear question, and make it easy to agree to a small next step. Concise messages are easier to scan and personalize.

4. Lead with value, not a pitch

Open with something useful: an informed observation, a relevant idea, or a resource. Earn attention before explaining what you sell.

5. Make the next step effortless

End with one clear request. “Worth a quick 15-minute call this week?” is easier to answer than an open-ended “Let me know your thoughts.”

6. Structure the email with AIDA

  • Attention: earn a second look with a relevant subject and specific opening.
  • Interest: explain why you're reaching out in a way that matters to the recipient.
  • Desire: offer a useful outcome, quick win, or credible proof.
  • Action: close with one clear, low-effort ask.

In the first email, use the full Attention–Interest–Desire–Action sequence. In the follow-up, briefly rebuild interest, add one useful detail, and repeat the action. This avoids leading with a pitch or ending without a clear next step.

Bringing it all together

A/B testing, email marketing, and cold outreach all rely on the same idea: pay attention to what actually works, not what you assume will work. Test your subject lines, send emails customers want to open, and keep new-prospect outreach concise, relevant, and value-led.

Small, consistent improvements compound over time—and that's what turns marketing from a guessing game into a growth engine.
